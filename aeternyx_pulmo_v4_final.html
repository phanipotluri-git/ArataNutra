Profile 3 — Environmental & Air Quality Exposure
Urban PM2.5 · Ozone / NOₓ · Biomass / indoor cooking smoke · Vehicular exhaust · Any patient in Indian Tier 1–3 cities with chronic AQI exposure above 100 µg/m³
Environmental Oxidative Pathobiology
India has 21 of the 30 most polluted cities globally (IQAir 2023). PM2.5 particles ≤2.5 µm penetrate to alveolar level, generating reactive oxygen species on bronchial epithelial surfaces that activate NF-κB within 30 minutes — triggering the same IL-6/IL-8/TNF-α cytokine cascade that drives COPD and lung cancer. Long-term PM2.5 exposure independently causes COPD in non-smokers, accelerates FEV1 decline, and increases lung cancer risk. This is your non-smoking, non-vaping patient with no obvious risk factor label — yet under daily oxidative pulmonary assault simply by commuting in Hyderabad, Delhi, or Mumbai.

Ozone (O₃) and NOₓ from vehicular exhaust react with airway lining fluid lipids to generate lipid peroxidation chain reactions that damage bronchial surfactant, drive mucus hypersecretion, and cause bronchospasm. Ozone exposure is independently associated with hospitalisation for COPD and asthma exacerbation.

Biomass and indoor cooking smoke (LPG, wood, crop residue) affects an estimated 700M Indians. Women who cook over biomass develop non-smoker COPD at rates approaching tobacco-attributable COPD. The small airway disease and diffusion capacity decline from indoor smoke is driven by the same NF-κB/oxidative pathway as tobacco smoke — without the social recognition as a risk factor. This is a profoundly underdiagnosed and underserved patient population.

Profile 4 — Autoimmune & Immune-Mediated Lung Conditions
RA-ILD · Sarcoidosis · Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis · Connective Tissue Disease-ILD (CTD-ILD) · Sjögren's lung · SLE pulmonary manifestations · Inflammatory airway disease
Autoimmune Pulmonary Oxidative Pathobiology
In autoimmune lung conditions, dysregulated immune cell activation generates a persistent, self-amplifying oxidative-inflammatory cycle in pulmonary tissue. In RA-ILD, activated fibroblast-like synoviocytes and alveolar macrophages produce sustained ROS that drives the TGF-β1-mediated fibrotic remodelling progressing to UIP or NSIP patterns. NF-κB is constitutively activated in RA synovial tissue and extends this inflammatory signal to lung parenchyma. In Sarcoidosis, alveolar macrophage accumulation generates ACE and pro-inflammatory cytokines — oxidative stress is a key amplifier of granuloma formation and persistence. In HP, antigen-driven T-lymphocyte activation and macrophage recruitment create persistent oxidative bursts in alveolar tissue — contributing to fibrotic progression after antigen withdrawal. In CTD-ILD (SSc, PM/DM, Sjögren's), mitochondrial dysfunction in lung fibroblasts and myofibroblast differentiation driven by TGF-β and oxidative stress are primary fibrotic mechanisms independent of autoantibody status. Selenium deficiency — highly prevalent in autoimmune conditions including thyroid disease, RA, and SLE — further impairs the GPx antioxidant system, amplifying oxidative damage in already-inflamed tissue.

Profile 5 — Sleep Disorders & Sleep-Related Oxidative Disease

Sleep Disorder Oxidative Pathobiology
OSA — Ischaemia-Reperfusion Cascade: Each apnoeic event creates a hypoxia-reoxygenation cycle that generates a burst of reactive oxygen species through xanthine oxidase activation and mitochondrial uncoupling — identical to the ischaemia-reperfusion injury pattern seen in cardiac surgery, occurring 15–100 times per hour in severe OSA. Urinary 8-isoprostane — the gold-standard lipid peroxidation biomarker — is elevated 3–4× above normal in moderate-severe OSA. CRP, IL-6, and TNF-α are chronically elevated. These drive the endothelial dysfunction, insulin resistance, hypertension, cardiac arrhythmias, and neurocognitive decline that define OSA's systemic complications. CPAP treats the apnoeic events — it does not fully normalise the oxidative burden in all patients (Grebe et al. 2006, AJRCCM).

Overlap Syndrome (OSA + COPD): The combination of COPD-related hypoxaemia and OSA-related intermittent hypoxia creates a compounded oxidative pulmonary burden that neither diagnosis alone predicts. Outcomes in overlap syndrome are significantly worse than either condition alone — Aeternyx's dual-pathway coverage of both conditions makes it particularly logical here.

Chronic Sleep Deprivation (<6 hrs/night): Independently elevates systemic IL-6, TNF-α, CRP, and urinary 8-isoprostane through circadian disruption of SIRT1/Nrf2 antioxidant gene expression — without any apnoeic events. The HITEC City software engineer working 12-hour days is accumulating oxidative burden that SIRT1 (Resveratrol) and CoQ10 specifically address.

Profile 6 — Occupational Lung Exposures
Silicosis · Coal workers' pneumoconiosis · Asbestosis · Welder's lung (siderosis) · Byssinosis · Agricultural lung (HP, mycotoxins) · Chemical plant / solvent exposure · India has 40M+ workers in high-exposure occupations
Occupational Oxidative Pathobiology
Occupational lung disease follows a characteristic dual-phase oxidative-fibrotic pattern: acute ROS phase — inhaled particles (silica, coal, metal fumes) activate alveolar macrophages to generate superoxide and hydrogen peroxide within hours; and chronic fibrotic phase — persistent macrophage activation triggers TGF-β1/Smad fibrotic remodelling, collagen I/III deposition, and progressive small airway obliteration.

Crystalline silica generates persistent superoxide in alveolar macrophages through frustrated phagocytosis — the silica particle cannot be digested, so the macrophage sustains ROS production indefinitely. This progressive oxidative drive is the mechanism of silicosis progression even after silica exposure ceases.

Welding fumes contain iron oxide, manganese, nickel, chromium — each a metal capable of Fenton reaction chemistry, generating hydroxyl radicals that cause direct DNA oxidative damage and NF-κB activation. Welder's siderosis progression to COPD and lung cancer is driven by this continuous metal-catalysed ROS production.

India's 40M+ occupationally exposed workers receive virtually no preventive cellular protection. These patients present to pulmonologists after fibrosis is already established. Aeternyx is most valuable as a preventive daily protocol for at-risk workers before disease onset, or as an adjunct to reduce progression once disease is established.

Safety Profile, Contraindications & Drug Interactions
Established Safety — All 10 Ingredients at These Doses
All 10 active ingredients are at doses well within established safety margins from regulatory agencies (FDA, EFSA, FSSAI) and peer-reviewed safety literature:

• Astaxanthin 4mg: GRAS status. Safety established up to 24mg/day. No toxicity at 4mg.
• Trans-Resveratrol 98% 100mg: Human clinical trials demonstrate safety up to 5,000mg/day. 100mg is a low-moderate dose with no reported adverse events at this level.
• CoQ10 100mg: Safety established up to 1,200mg/day. 100mg has an extensive multi-decade human safety record.
• ALA 50mg: Therapeutic doses in diabetic neuropathy studies: 600–1,800mg/day. 50mg is a preventive dose with no safety concerns.
• Tocotrienol 10mg: Safety established up to 400mg/day. 10mg is a conservative protective dose.
• Selenium 40mcg: Tolerable upper intake level: 400mcg/day. 40mcg is the WHO recommended nutritional dose — far from any toxicity concern.
• Lycopene 4mg, Vit C 40mg, Vit D3 600 IU, K2-7 MK-7 45mcg: All at preventive nutritional doses with established safety records.
Caution — Drug Interactions to Monitor
Warfarin / Acenocoumarol: Vitamin K2-7 MK-7 at 45mcg may mildly influence INR. Monitor INR at initiation and at 4 weeks. Dose adjustment of anticoagulant may be required in some patients. Caution warranted — not an absolute contraindication.

High-dose antiplatelet / thrombolytics: Resveratrol has mild antiplatelet activity at high doses. At 100mg this is not clinically significant, but theoretical caution is appropriate in patients on dual antiplatelet therapy or thrombolysis.

No significant interactions documented with: SABA/LABA bronchodilators · Inhaled corticosteroids · Oral corticosteroids · Montelukast · Pirfenidone / Nintedanib · PAH therapies (ERA, PDE5i, prostacyclins) · CPAP therapy · Standard COPD triple therapy · Antibiotics · Immunosuppressants (mycophenolate, azathioprine) · Methotrexate · Oxygen therapy
